Design

Great design happens when visual attributes and functional qualities get to enhance one another. You could say, I hang on to my Scandinavian roots.

When I design I try to create something so simple, so intuitive, that it altogether seems… completely obvious.

It’s about agile & efficient processes, a vision and strategy based on true understanding of your customers. I like to be a driving force in making that happen.

user research

Users and their behaviors are my main source of inspiration as a designer. The search for knowledge and inspiration challenges my imagination and triggers my creativity.

I use various methods to engage with users such as context mapping, cultural probes, creative sessions, semi-structured interviews, interactive questionnaires and observations. I evaluate designs and concepts through usability and user experience testing. For market research I do competitive analyses, competitor reviews and case studies.

Context mapping

I use creative materials and exercises to reach a deeper understanding for implicit and tacit user needs. Sensitizing materials allows for self-reflection and prepares the participants for a creative session where main insights are gathered.

More about the method (Sleeswijk Visser et al, 2005)

Cultural Probes

I use small design packages to quickly gather inspirational data about people’s lives, behaviors and values. Depending on the topic, I create maps, postcards, dairies etc. along with creative tasks that I send to (and later collect from) my participants.

Sensitizing booklet / Context Mapping Skills TU Delft for Wacom 2012

Creative sessions

I facilitate small groups in creativity. Models, posters, concepts etc. are made and presented while I guide the participants through generative exercises and motivate them to share their views, thoughts and feelings around a specific topic.

Usability testing

During a usability evaluation I measure criteria such as learnability, efficiency and user satisfaction. Participants are asked to perform tasks while imagining different scenarios. Observations are made, notes are taken and effort ratings etc. are recorded.

Usability and UX Assessment / TU Delft for Océ 2012

UX evaluation

User experiences are subjective, context-dependent and changing over time. I use techniques such as observations, think-out-loud protocols, EmoCards and AttrakDiff questionnaires to evaluate User experiences.

Usability and UX Assessment / TU Delft for Océ 2012

Interviews

I often use semi-structured interviews in combination with other methods to further deepen my understanding and allow for additional user comments. The method is especially useful when exploring general (as opposite to specific) user attitudes and thoughts.

Competitive analysis

I use competitive analysis to determine how a site performs in relation to its competition. Expert reviews or competitive usability tests are made to compare over-all usability metrics or specific features and design elements across different sites.

Extract from competitive analysis for Whatser 2014

Competitor review

I like to orient myself in a new field by making a competitor review. Many important insights can be gathered simply by identifying the competition - the size and scope of the market.

Competitor review, part of MSc Graduation Project / TU Delft for Exact, 2013

Case & Field Study

By making case & field studies I get to explore and communicate potentials and boundaries related to a specific context. It’s a great method for setting the direction of a project and discover unmet user needs while observing real user behaviors.

Case study, part of Joint Master Project / TU Delft for Bluerise 2012

analyze

Analyzing qualitative data frequently means structuring big amounts of complex data. I often find it useful to (for a short while) emerge myself in the data and focus completely on understanding and processing the information.

It is a step-wise process typically starting with a transcribing phase where I study the research materials and note down user comments. I explore the transcripts further by highlight especially interesting quotes that I translate into user insights. These insights can then be clustered according to various criteria, and by organizing the clusters user patterns and/or conflicts can be identified in the information.

communicate

User insights are nice – but useless on their own. I get to use all my creativity when bringing them alive. The mediums I use depend on the information I strive to convey and the people I am talking with. Each method supports the concept development process and communication with stakeholders in a different way. The overall purpose is to motivate consistent design decisions throughout the design & development cycle.

I often start on a abstract, conceptual level - mapping out business strategies and overall goals. I thereafter bring the high-level ideas to more and more detail, describing the users and their behaviors & needs in the form of personas, customer journeys and storyboards.

Strategy diagrams

A good user experience starts with a clearly defined strategy, which leads to a good understanding for and communication of underlying goals. This can be in the form of infographics describing higher-level company visions or business models.

Strategy diagram, part of MSc Graduation Project / TU Delft for Exact, 2013

Conceptual models

Conceptual models help in developing and communicating a clear understanding for the vision of a product. I use the method early on in the design process and often implement metaphors or descriptive illustrations to demonstrate my ideas.

Conceptual model, part of MSc Graduation Project / TU Delft for Exact, 2013

Metaphors

I use metaphors as a way to create a mental picture and give a ‘personality’ to the product I am developing. The metaphor guides me in the entire design process and can support the decision making process in a similar way as personas do.

Metaphor, part of Usability and UX Assessment / TU Delft for Océ 2012

Personas

By creating user profiles I give your users a name and face within the company. Such a seemingly small thing can greatly support a more user-focused work process and enable discussions where the end-user is directly taken into account.

Persona, part of MSc Graduation Project / TU Delft for Exact, 2013

User scenarios

Typical user situations can be described in user scenarios. It is a simple method for conveying user needs and experiences while adding context to your personas.

User scenario / TU Delft for Wacom, 2012

Storyboards

Story telling is great for describing user experiences. I often communicate my stories in the form of storyboards where the user interaction is clearly described with text and images.

Storyboard, part of MSc Graduation Project / TU Delft for Exact, 2013

Costumer journeys

When I need to map out sequential information I use the method of customer journeys. They are perfect for describing, for example, various product interactions and mapping these to different user needs.

Customer Journey, part of MSc Graduation Project / TU Delft for Exact, 2013

Mindmaps

For illustrating groups of information and their internal relationships, mindmapping works very well. I use it as a creative tool while analyzing as well as communicating insights.

Mindmap, part of Joint Master Project / TU Delft for Bluerise 2012

conceptualize

After having gained a clear understanding for a specific user group and context it is finally time to bring the insights further and start creating something truly useful.

The methods I use to explore and convey concept ideas vary, most of the time depending on the scope of the project. In the case of completely new concepts I often start by creating initial sketches and map out the overall functionality in architecture & functional diagrams. To define more detailed interactions I create wireframes, interactive and physical prototypes and if needed visual style-guides and storyboards.

Architecture diagrams

I use architecture diagrams or sitemaps to illustrate how a concept is organized and structured. It defines what parts (such as web-pages) are included and how the different content and functionality fit together to form a cohesive whole.

Functional diagrams

After defining the architecture I typically move on to create a more detailed description of the core functionality. What services are provided within each module - what are the specific needs and problems to be fulfilled and solved for the user?

Concept sketches

In the early idea generation process I use pen and paper to explore different ideas. Quick sketches are perfect as a base for discussion before moving on to digital tools.

Wireframes

I like to work iteratively, in quick development cycles. Digital tools such as Balsamiq or Axure are flexible and allow me to easily update my designs. I use wireframes both as a way to do quick internal testing and to communicate final concepts to the development team.

Product flowcharts

When communicating my designs to the development team, and further support their continued work, I often include an overview flowchart mapping out the relation between different wireframes.

Interactive prototypes

To better understand the user experience and test various solutions I develop wireframes further into interactive prototypes. Simple prototypes are also great for communicating ideas within the team.

Support starting a business, part of MSc Graduation Project / TU Delft for Exact, 2013

Physical prototypes

When working with physical products, physical prototypes are used to evaluate size, material, shape etc. I also use quick methods such as paper prototyping to quickly simulate digital concepts and test them early on in the development process.

Expression boards

I make various visual boards that serve as inspirations but also as a tool to communicate the expression I aim for in terms of style and interactions.

Visual style-guides

I am not a graphical designer, but while designing the user experience I also develop ideas for the visual appearance. I make style-guides to communicate my vision and support the visual design process.
